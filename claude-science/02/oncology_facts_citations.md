# Primary literature for three established oncology facts

## EGFR T790M defeats first-generation TKIs

The T790M "gatekeeper" mutation was pinned down as the cause of acquired resistance to first-generation EGFR inhibitors in two back-to-back 2005 reports. [Kobayashi et al. 2005](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa044238) identified a secondary EGFR mutation, T790M, in a patient whose NSCLC relapsed on gefitinib, and showed in transfected cells that T790M restores kinase activity in the presence of drug — resistance at the level of the target itself rather than a bypass pathway. In parallel, [Pao et al. 2005](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.0020073) sequenced erlotinib- and gefitinib-resistant lung adenocarcinomas and found the same T790M substitution, demonstrating that the mutation prevents these reversible inhibitors from suppressing EGFR signaling. Both are translational/clinical reports anchored by mechanistic cell-based confirmation; together they established T790M as the dominant on-target resistance mechanism to erlotinib and gefitinib.

## Osimertinib was engineered to overcome T790M

The third-generation inhibitor osimertinib (AZD9291) was designed specifically to retain potency against T790M. [Cross et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.1158/2159-8290.CD-14-0337) reported the preclinical characterization: an irreversible, mutant-selective EGFR-TKI that covalently engages Cys797 and potently inhibits both activating and T790M-mutant EGFR in cell lines and xenografts while largely sparing wild-type receptor, limiting the toxicity that caps first-generation dosing. The clinical proof came from the first-in-human AURA study, [Jänne et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1411817), in which AZD9291 produced high and durable response rates concentrated in T790M-positive, TKI-resistant NSCLC — directly validating the T790M-overcoming design in patients.

## Sotorasib and adagrasib covalently target KRAS G12C at Cys12

The therapeutic strategy against KRAS G12C rests on the mutant cysteine itself. [Ostrem et al. 2013](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature12796) provided the original proof-of-concept, showing that small molecules can form a covalent bond with Cys12 of KRAS(G12C) and, through co-crystal structures, defining the previously unrecognized switch-II allosteric pocket that these compounds exploit to lock the GTPase in its inactive state. That mechanism was carried into the two approved drugs: [Canon et al. 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1694-1) characterized AMG 510 (sotorasib) as a covalent Cys12-targeting inhibitor with structural and in vivo antitumor data, and [Hallin et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1158/2159-8290.CD-19-1167) did the same for MRTX849 (adagrasib), demonstrating selective covalent inhibition across G12C-mutant tumor models with early patient responses. The medicinal-chemistry discovery report for adagrasib, [Fell et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jmedchem.9b02052), details the covalent Cys12 binding mode crystallographically. All four are preclinical/structural reports; the clinical efficacy that followed is documented separately in the CodeBreaK and KRYSTAL trial programs.
